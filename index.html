Project 9

Implementation of an Index-3 Differential-Algebraic Equation Solver on Parallel Architecture

Objective: The Absolute Nodal Coordinate Formulation (ANCF) has been widely used to carry out the dynamics analysis of flexible bodies that undergo large rotation and large deformation. This formulation is consistent with the nonlinear theory of continuum mechanics and is computationally more efficient compared to other nonlinear finite element formulations. Kinematic constraints that represent mechanical joints and specified motion trajectories can be introduced to make complex flexible mechanisms. As the complexity of a mechanism increases, the system of differential algebraic equations becomes very large and results in a computational bottleneck.

Method: This contribution helps alleviate this bottleneck using three tools: (1) an implicit time-stepping algorithm, (2) fine-grained parallel processing on the Graphics Processing Unit (GPU), and (3) enabling parallelism through a novel Constraint-Based Mesh (CBM) approach. The combination of these tools results in a fast solution process that scales linearly for large numbers of elements, allowing meaningful engineering problems to be solved.

Contributors: Daniel Melanz, Naresh Khude, Paramsothy Jayakumar, Dan Negrut

Project 8

Simulation of a Flexible Tire using Absolute Nodal Coordinate Plate Elements and Bilateral Constraints

Objective: Model a flexible tire on rigid terrain using the ANCF.

Method: A rolling tire model was created in the sequential version of the ANCF simulation software. The simulated tire has dimensions of 0.4318 m, 0.635 m, and 0.0127 m for the inner diameter, outer diameter, and thickness, respectively. The tire has a weight of 120 N, a Poisson’s ratio of 0.3, and a friction coefficient of 0.3. The model is composed of 10 ANCF gradient deficient plate elements with 1200 contact points per plate. The tire is exposed to a gravitational force of 9.81 m/s2 and contact forces from the ground. The ANCF system uses a time step of 1e-4 seconds and an iteration tolerance of 1e-4. The elastic modulus of the tire was set to 3e8 Pa and a torque of 800 N-m was applied to induce rolling in the tire.

Contributors: Daniel Melanz, Naresh Khude, Paramsothy Jayakumar, Dan Negrut

Project 6

Parallelization of the Absolute Nodal Coordinate Formulation for Flexible Dynamics

Objective: Although ANCF has several features that make it computationally efficient, many calculations must be performed to update the position, velocity, and acceleration during a single time step. This computational effort can be alleviated using parallel architecture, such as the Graphical Processing Unit or a cluster, to compute several tasks simultaneously. This approach provides a framework for parallelizing several ANCF elements.

Method: This contribution discusses how a flexible body formalism, ANCF, is combined with a frictional/contact model using the Discrete Element Method (DEM) to address many-body dynamics problems; i.e., problems with hundreds of thousands of rigid and deformable bodies. Since the computational effort associated with these problems is significant, the analytical framework is implemented to leverage the computational power available on today’s commodity Graphical Processing Unit (GPU) cards. The code developed is validated against ANSYS and FEAP results. The resulting simulation capability is demonstrated in conjunction with hair simulation.

Contributors: Daniel Melanz, Naresh Khude, Ilinca Stanciulescu, Dan Negrut

Project 5

Investigation of a Landing Robot on Near Earth Objects

Objective: Simulate the landing of a robotic hopping device on a Near Earth Object (NEO), such as an asteroid.

Method: The open-source multi-body dynamics simulation tool Chrono::Engine, developed by Professor Alessandro Tasora of the University of Parma, Italy, formulates a physics model as a differential variational inequality (DVI), and uses an iterative approach to solve for the accelerations and impulses of all bodies in the system at each time step. Time integration is used to compute the associated velocities and positions. The validation process detailed in this report compares the known solution of several physics cases against a corresponding Chrono::Engine simulation output. These physics cases test the ability of Chrono::Engine to handle specific physical phenomena, including: collisions and multiple contacts, conservation of momentum, and the solution of statically indeterminate structures. By testing several different isolated physical phenomena, a comprehensive test suite will be created for debugging complex multi-body dynamics models and regression testing. This research was carried out at the Jet Propulsion Laboratory, California Institute of Technology, and was sponsored by Space Grant and the National Aeronautics and Space Administration.

Contributors: Daniel Melanz, Alessandro Tasora, Bob Balaram, Abhinandan Jain, Dan Negrut

Project 3

Validation of a Differential Variational Inequality Approach for the Dynamics of Granular Material

Objective: Validate a DVI approach for the dynamics of granular material by comparing the experimental and simulation results of granular flow for two tests in the Chrono::Engine simulation environment.

Method: For this work, an aluminum rig was designed and fabricated to measure the flow rate of a given amount of micro scale granular material flowing due to gravity through a slit. The flow was initiated by using a Newport UMR8.25 translational stage and Newport LTA-HL precision linear actuator to open and close the slit steadily. Once the slit was open, the weight of the granular material was transmitted to the processor via a router connected to a Cooper LFS242 Tension/Compression Cell (Serial No. 286284) and graphed over time. A model of the flow meter was created in Chrono::Engine and the results were matched to experimental runs by changing the friction coefficient between particles. After the friction coefficient of the particles was determined to be 0.15, several experimental runs with differing slit sizes were run. These flow rates were compared to the weight versus time data that Chrono::Engine output for the corresponding slit size. Runs for gap sizes of 1.5mm, 2.0mm, 2.5mm and 3.0mm were performed with 0.0624 N of granular material, which amounted to approximately 39,000 spheres with 500μm in diameter. These gap sizes corresponded to an experimental flow rate of 1.41E-2 N/s, 2.59E-2 N/s, 4.00E-2 N/s, and 4.44E-2 N/s, and a simulated flow rate of 1.40E-2 N/s, 2.62E-2 N/s, 4.05E-2 N/s, and 4.48E-2 N/s, respectively. Based on this experiment, Chrono::Engine had less than a 2% error in calculating the flow rate of the granular material through a slit. In addition to comparing flow rates, the pile repose angle from the experimental runs was compared to the simulation results. A description of the GPU execution model along with its memory spaces is provided to illustrate its potential for parallel scientific computing. The equations of motion associated with the dynamics of many rigid bodies are introduced and a solution method is presented. The solution method is designed to map well on the parallel hardware, which is demonstrated by an order of magnitude reductions in simulation time for large systems that concern the dynamics of granular material.

Contributors: Daniel Melanz, Martin Tupy, Alessandro Tasora, Hammad Mazhar, Bryce Smith, Kevin Turner, Dan Negrut

Project 1

Investigation of Digging Forces on an Electric Mining Shovel

Objective: Develop a tool that can be used to run virtual experiments on an excavator. This tool should set up a dynamic simulation to calculate minimum cycle time of an electric shovel based on modifications of input data and input geometry. Inputs include, motor parameters, gear ratios, mass, and inertia.

Method: Model the P&H 4100XPC using ADAMS to accurately simulate the kinematics and dynamics of the shovel and Matlab/Simulink to simulate the electric motors. Perform a co-simulation with Chrono::Engine to model digging through a bank.

Contributors: Daniel Melanz, Justin Madsen, Martin Tupy, Dan Negrut, Alessandro Tasora
